# Wildton — Field Lore (Frida)

A small field-lore collection representing quiet, practical knowledge Frida carries as part of daily life. These entries describe *known things*, not secrets, spells, or personal memories.

---

## Starcap Mushrooms

**Type:** Cultivated Fungal Plant\
**Region:** Mountain slopes near Wildton

Starcap mushrooms grow best in cool, shaded soil near stone and old roots. Their dark caps carry pale speckling that resembles faint star patterns when seen at dusk.

Frida cultivates Starcaps in a small cave near her home, keeping the beds damp and undisturbed. She does not use magic in their care, relying instead on routine, timing, and steady temperature.

She knows them only because Magus told her about them and showed her how to tend the beds. To Frida, they are rare and personal rather than part of local knowledge.

When the cave is quiet, the caps sometimes seem to hum, whisper, or sway as if keeping time with one another. Frida treats this as a harmless quirk and enjoys it, the way one enjoys a joke or a half-remembered song. She has never tried to explain it to anyone else.

Frida understands only that Magus uses the mushrooms for travel, and does not ask further.

## Moonwhite Owl Figurine

**Type:** Hand-Carved Figurine\
**Material:** Pale wood or bone-like resin (origin unclear)

A small figurine depicting a moonwhite owl with closed wings and wide, watchful eyes. The surface is smooth and cool to the touch, catching light softly rather than reflecting it.

Magus gave the figurine to Frida as a quiet gift and told her the owl is called **Noctessa**. Frida knows nothing more than the name and the owl’s moonwhite aspect.

She keeps the figurine near her sleeping space or on a shelf where it can observe the room.

At times, Frida has the sense that the figurine is *present* rather than inert — not watching her, but sharing the same stillness. This feeling comes most often during half-waking moments, daydreams, or long pauses before sleep, when thoughts loosen and drift.

She does not interpret this as a message, vision, or dream. It feels closer to awareness brushing against awareness, faint and easily ignored. Frida accepts it without trying to name it.

The figurine is not known to act or move. Frida believes it represents protection, guidance, or remembrance rather than an active charm. She treats it with care but not reverence.

Some locals say owl symbols encourage patience and listening, especially during uncertain seasons.

## Hearthroot Balm

**Type:** Herbal Salve\
**Region:** Common in Wildton households

Hearthroot balm is made from crushed hearthroot leaves mixed with animal fat or oil and gently warmed. The resulting salve carries a mild, earthy scent.

It is used for small cuts, sore joints, and cold-stiffened hands. The effect is practical rather than dramatic, easing discomfort and restoring circulation over time.

Frida learned to prepare hearthroot balm from her aunt and often keeps a small jar on hand. Sharing it is considered a gesture of care rather than medicine.

In Wildton, a home without hearthroot balm is seen as poorly tended, regardless of wealth.

## Ashleaf Drying Rack

**Type:** Household Structure\
**Region:** Common in mountain cottages around Wildton

A simple wooden frame strung with thin cords, used for air-drying herbs, roots, and small fungi. Ashleaf branches are favored for the frame, as they resist mold and hold their shape through seasonal damp.

The rack is usually placed near a hearth or sheltered wall where air circulates gently. Proper spacing matters more than heat; overcrowded racks spoil quickly.

Frida uses her drying rack year-round, adjusting what hangs there with the seasons. A well-kept rack is taken as a sign of attentiveness rather than abundance.

In Wildton, an empty rack in harvest season draws quiet concern.

## Button Jar

**Type:** Household Container\
**Material:** Glass jar or small tin

A simple jar filled with mismatched buttons collected over many years. Some are carved wood, some bone, some dull metal; a few have lost their shanks and rattle softly when the jar is moved.

The buttons come from mended coats, traded garments, lost travelers, and items no one remembers owning. None are valuable on their own.

Frida keeps the jar near her work table and often sorts through it while thinking. Choosing the *right* button for a repair is considered a small but telling decision.

In Wildton, offering someone a button from your jar is a quiet sign of trust: it means you expect them to return something worth fixing.
